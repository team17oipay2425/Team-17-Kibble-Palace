IR Sensors

We explored the use of dual infrared break beam sensors at the entrance to the Kibble Palace to achieve accurate directional counting.

System Demonstration

Our dual infrared break beam sensor system represents a breakthrough in heritage-compatible visitor counting technology. Through extensive testing and refinement, we developed a solution that achieves high accuracy in distinguishing between entry and exit movements while remaining completely invisible to visitors.

The system operates on sequential beam interruption logic: when a visitor enters, they first break beam A, then beam B (A→B = entry). Conversely, exiting visitors trigger the sequence B→A (exit). This directional awareness eliminates the common problem of simple counting systems that cannot differentiate between arrivals and departures.

Technical Implementation

Our sensors utilise infrared light beams positioned at knee height to detect movement patterns. The choice of infrared technology ensures the system works reliably in all lighting conditions, from the bright natural light filtering through the Kibble Palace's glass structure to evening hours when artificial lighting takes over.

The sensors communicate wirelessly with a central processing unit, eliminating the need for visible cables that would compromise the heritage aesthetic. Battery-powered operation means no electrical work is required, respecting the building's listed status while ensuring reliable operation for months between maintenance visits.

QR Code Implementation

Understanding visitor demographics is crucial for Glasgow City Council's strategic planning, funding applications, and resource allocation. Our optional QR code system bridges the gap between respecting visitor privacy and gathering valuable demographic insights that inform decision-making.

The system employs strategically placed QR codes that link to a simple, mobile-optimized form collecting basic demographic information: country of origin, age range, and visit purpose. This data helps the Council understand their international appeal, plan for peak seasons, and demonstrate impact to funding bodies.

Implementation Strategy

QR codes are positioned at natural pause points throughout the visitor journey - near information boards, seating areas, and plant displays where visitors naturally stop to read or rest. This organic integration ensures high visibility without being intrusive or disrupting the contemplative atmosphere of the glasshouse.

The linked form is designed with user experience principles, requiring less than 30 seconds to complete. Multiple language options accommodate international visitors, while the mobile-first design ensures compatibility across all device types.

Data Visualization Dashboard

A comprehensive dashboard designed to help staff make informed decisions about operations and resource allocation.

Dashboard Development

The dashboard transforms raw sensor data into actionable insights for Glasgow City Council staff. Designed through extensive user research with facilities managers, horticultural staff, and administrative personnel, the interface prioritizes clarity and practical utility over complex analytics.

Real-time occupancy data helps staff make immediate decisions about opening additional areas during peak times or adjusting climate control systems based on visitor load. Historical trend analysis reveals seasonal patterns, enabling better staff scheduling and maintenance planning during quieter periods.

Physical Testing

To better simulate visitor traffic patterns in the Kibble Palace, we created physical mock-ups for testing sensor placement and accuracy.

Physical Testing Methods

To validate our sensor placement strategy and counting accuracy, we constructed detailed cardboard mock-ups of the Kibble Palace entrance areas. This hands-on testing phase proved crucial in identifying potential issues before installation and optimizing the system for real-world conditions.

The physical prototypes allowed us to simulate various visitor scenarios: individual visitors, family groups, children running ahead, elderly visitors with mobility aids, and staff members carrying equipment. Each scenario was tested multiple times to ensure consistent accuracy across different movement patterns and speeds.

Comprehensive Testing Methodology

Our testing protocol included over 200 individual test scenarios covering single visitors, couples, family groups up to 8 people, wheelchair users, visitors with pushchairs, and groups with mixed walking speeds. We specifically tested challenging scenarios like children running back and forth, visitors stopping directly between sensors, and simultaneous bidirectional traffic.

Video analysis from multiple angles allowed us to verify sensor accuracy against actual visitor counts. This rigorous approach identified that optimal sensor spacing is 1.2 meters apart, positioned 15cm above ground level for maximum accuracy while avoiding interference from pushchairs or bags.
